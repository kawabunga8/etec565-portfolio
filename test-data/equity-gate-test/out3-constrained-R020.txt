1. Areas to Explore
Direct Evidence & Signs of Agency

The student writes that they learned a "secondary instrument that I learned this year" and spent "a lot of time practicing independently and finding connections between what I already knew and the skills required for the new instrument." This suggests self-direction in bridging their prior knowledge with a new domain.

They also outline a specific, self-driven goal to "become more comfortable improvising," noting that they currently "rely heavily on written music or memorized pieces" and plan to develop this by "learning scales, listening closely to other musicians, and practicing with accompaniment tracks."

Competing Explanations for Key Behaviors

The pivot to a secondary instrument:

Interpretation A: This represents genuine, self-chosen agency where the student took the initiative to learn a new instrument to challenge themselves.

Interpretation B: This was a pragmatic response to an ensemble need (e.g., the band director needed someone to play a specific part) or a curriculum requirement, and the student's "agency" was simply a highly cooperative adaptation to an assigned task.

To distinguish these: The teacher needs to ask who initiated the switch to the secondary instrument and what the student's internal motivation was for doing so.

Problem-solving "collaboratively with classmates":

Interpretation A: The student actively sought out peers to co-create solutions to technical musical challenges, demonstrating relational agency.

Interpretation B: This occurred during structured, teacher-mandated peer-review sessions where collaboration was a graded or highly monitored requirement.

To distinguish these: The teacher must find out whether these discussions happened spontaneously outside of structured class time or during planned classroom activities.

Unclear Areas & Gaps

Which instruments are involved: The student never names their primary or secondary instrument. It is unknown if they transitioned between highly similar instruments (e.g., flute to piccolo) or vastly different families (e.g., percussion to brass), which significantly changes the nature of the "different techniques" and "habits" they had to build.

What "practiced consistently" actually looked like: The student mentions they "practiced consistently, took breaks when needed, and focused extra attention on difficult passages," but the actual structure, frequency, and emotional toll of this independent practice remain unstated.

Task Completion vs. Genuine Agency

The progress note regarding "sight-reading skills" reads more like task completion. The student notes that "preparing a large amount of music within a shorter timeline forced me to become more confident." The word "forced" suggests an external compliance pressure rather than an internally driven pursuit, even though the resulting skill development was positive.

2. Follow-Up Questions

"You mentioned finding connections between your primary instrument and the secondary one you learned this year. What were those two instruments, and what was a specific connection you found that made the transition easier?"

"When you were working on those difficult passages and chose to 'take breaks when needed,' how did you decide when a break was necessary versus when to keep pushing through?"

"You wrote about wanting to learn to improvise by 'listening closely to other musicians.' Who are the musicians you are listening to right now, and what is a specific element in their playing that you want to bring into your own music?"

"You noted that you solved performance-related problems by 'discussing strategies for improvement' with classmates. Can you describe one specific problem you and a classmate ran into, and how you worked together to solve it?"

3. Feedback Paragraph

In your reflection, you clearly detailed the specific steps you took to learn your secondary instrument, particularly how you actively looked for connections to your existing musical knowledge and chose to focus extra attention on difficult passages. Your plan to transition toward improvisation by studying scales, using accompaniment tracks, and analyzing other musicians gives you a concrete roadmap for directing your own progress. As you move forward, try applying those same problem-solving strategies you used with your classmates to your solo improvisation practice, perhaps by sharing your early, unscripted drafts with a peer to get their input on your dynamics and phrasing.
