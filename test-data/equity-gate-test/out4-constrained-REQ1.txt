Areas to Explore
Direct Evidence of Agency & Self-Direction

Taking a break and returning: The student noted, "When a part wouldn't work I'd take a break and come back to it..." This indicates a self-directed pacing strategy during independent practice.

Repetition: The student wrote that they would "just play it over and over until it stuck" and "practiced a lot on my own until the hard parts worked."

Designing a future learning strategy: To transition away from sheet music, the student planned a concrete action: "I'm going to try playing along with backing tracks."

Collaborative problem-solving: The student explicitly wrote, "Me and my friends helped each other figure out the hard parts," indicating they sought relational support rather than relying solely on teacher intervention.

Inferences as Competing Alternatives

Learning a second instrument: The student wrote, "I learned a second instrument this year."

Alternative A: This could suggest a highly self-motivated, curious pursuit chosen entirely by the student to challenge themselves outside of class requirements.

Alternative B: This could suggest a pragmatic response to a structural constraint, such as the year-end production requiring a specific instrument that no one else could play, meaning the student stepped up out of necessity rather than personal curiosity.

To distinguish these: The teacher needs to discover whether the student initiated the instrument change independently or if it was suggested/required for the production.

Repetition strategy ("over and over"): The student noted they played difficult parts "over and over until it stuck."

Alternative A: This could suggest a deliberate, patient commitment to building muscle memory through deliberate practice.

Alternative B: This could suggest a lack of alternative practice strategies, where the student defaulted to brute-force repetition because they didn't know how to deconstruct or analyze the mistake.

To distinguish these: The teacher needs to ask how the student decided when a part was "stuck" and what they did during those repetitions.

What is Unclear

It is unknown which two instruments the student is playing (the "main one" and the "second one").

It is unclear how much of the "practice on my own" happened during class time versus outside of school.

The reflection does not clarify if "making stuff up without sheet music" refers to improvisation, playing by ear, or original composition.

Task Completion vs. Genuine Agency

The statement "My reading got faster this year because we had a lot of songs and not much time" sounds like an adaptation to an external constraint (the pace of the class curriculum) rather than an internally driven goal.

The comment "Teacher feedback: Class was good. People helped each other out" appears to be a minimal response to a prompt, focusing on general class climate rather than a deep, personal evaluation of the instruction or their own growth.

Follow-Up Questions for the Teacher

"You mentioned that learning your second instrument was hard at first because you kept mixing it up with your main one. What are the two instruments, and what was a specific moment or technique where you realized you had finally stopped mixing them up?"

"When a tough part wouldn't work and you decided to 'take a break and come back to it,' what did you do during those breaks, and how did your mind or hands feel different when you sat back down to play?"

"You wrote that you and your friends helped each other figure out the hard parts. Can you walk me through one specific 'hard part' in a song where you and a friend sat down and cracked the code together?"

"To help you transition to playing without sheet music, you planned to use backing tracks. What kind of music or specific tracks are you planning to start with, and what is the very first step you'll take when the music starts playing?"

Feedback for the Student

When you ran into obstacles with the new fingerings on your second instrument, you chose to manage your own practice by taking intentional breaks and using focused repetition to build muscle memory. Rather than working entirely in isolation, you also paired up with your peers to actively break down and solve the most difficult parts of the music together. As you transition toward your goal of playing without sheet music, you have already identified a concrete strategy of practicing alongside backing tracks. To build on this plan, try starting with a very simple three-chord track and practice finding just the root notes by ear before attempting to build full melodies over the top.
